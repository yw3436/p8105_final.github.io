HIV Diagnoses in New York City

Investigating the HIV prelavence and factors associated with HIV

Yuqi Wang (yw3436); Rongqing Jia (rj2579); Xiaoyu Li (xl2834); Yan Song (ys3295)

Motivation

According to the latest report from New York Department of Health and Mental Hygiene HIV Epidemiology Program, there was 197 new HIV diagnoses and 1683 deaths among people with HIV (8.6 per 1000 people with HIV) in 2018. We would like to investigate the general trend of HIV diagnosis from 2011 to 2019, and also analysis the potential influence factors of it. In this project, we examined the trend of HIV prevalence and incidence from 2011 to 2019. In particular, we are interested in:

  • What is the general trend of HIV diagnoses across the time?
  • What is the demographic of the HIV population?
  • Are HIV diagnses different among different boroughs?
  • Whether unemployment rate and crime rate contribute to HIV prevalence.

Data Resources

We used data from the following datasets in NYC OpenData:

  • HIV/AIDS Annual Surveillance Statistics Annual data are provided on new diagnoses of HIV (non-AIDS), concurrent HIV/AIDS and AIDS, as well as the number of persons living with HIV and AIDS and the number of deaths among persons with HIV and AIDS. The data are shown for New York City overall and by sex, race, age, borough, area-based poverty level, risk factor, and United Hospital Fund neighborhood. All the data used in this project was extracted from the PDF of the annual reports.

  • Unemployment Rate Unemployment information for all boroughs in New York City (2015-2019) from New York State Deparmtent of Labor.

  • Crime Data Citywide crime data are provided by New York City Police Department (NYPD). The data show annual number of seven major felony offenses (murder and non-negligent manslaughter, rape, robbery, felony assault, burglary, grand larceny and grand larceny of a vehicle) by precinct (2013-2019).

  • Census Data Census data are provided on neighborhood tabulation area level by Department of City Planning (DCP). Since census takes place every 10 years, we used total population in 2010 census to calculate all rate measures.
